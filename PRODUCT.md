# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Political-science students and classroom facilitators use EconWar during a guided strategy session. Players choose departments, read regional clues, vote, spend Political Capital, and allocate money across Thailand's four regions under hidden economic phases.

## Product Purpose

EconWar teaches political economy through a phase-based game loop: read indicators, elect a Market Controller, allocate capital, use department abilities, and watch settlement results. Success means the interface makes the rules readable while preserving uncertainty and making financial outcomes feel like a warm civic harvest instead of a cold trading terminal.

## Brand Personality

Warm, civic, playful. The product should feel like a sunlit Thai town running a market festival: strategic enough for classroom debate, clear enough for first-time players, and cozy enough to invite experimentation.

## Anti-references

Do not make it look like a SaaS dashboard, stock-finance app, neon dark terminal, purple-gradient game UI, or sterile signup flow. Avoid decorative chips, random glow, Lucide-style default icons, cold charts as the primary read, and thin border-only selected states.

## Design Principles

Show the economy as physical game objects: coin jars, teal tokens, ledgers, banners, district tiles, town notices, and festival scoreboards.

Preserve hidden information. Before settlement, phase language must stay uncertain and use rumors, indicators, or bulletins rather than revealing truth.

Keep the two currencies unmistakable. Money is always gold; Political Capital is always teal.

Make every step answer the player's next decision: what changed, who controls the market, what can I spend, where can I allocate, and what happened at settlement.

Use warmth without losing legibility. Pixel materials and friendly copy support the strategy; they must not obscure numbers, controls, or state.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target readable classroom use on desktop and mobile. Preserve keyboard focus rings, strong text contrast, reduced-motion support, semantic form controls, and color-plus-text cues so players do not need color alone to understand state.
